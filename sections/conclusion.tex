\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

Two questions stand behind this thesis. The first is whether the derivative-based
theory of regular languages still works once exact symbol matching is dropped and
symbols match by similarity instead. The second is whether the core of that theory
can be checked by a proof assistant rather than trusted on paper. The preceding
sections answer yes to both.

On the crisp side the thesis develops derivatives of
regular languages and of regular expressions. Everything else rests on
Theorem~\ref{the:two-derivative-mapping}. It says that the syntactic derivative
of an expression and the semantic derivative of its language describe the same
thing once an expression is read as the language it denotes. From there derivatives
stay regular (Theorem~\ref{the:language-derivative-regular}) and an expression has only
finitely many derivative languages (Theorem~\ref{the:derivative-set-is-finite}).
That finiteness makes the derivative automaton possible. Taking
canonical derivatives as states and differentiation as the transition yields a
deterministic automaton that recognises the right language
(Theorem~\ref{the:dfa-correctness}) and that a terminating algorithm
builds.

In the fuzzy layer equality of symbols is replaced by a fuzzy similarity relation
together with a cut value $\mu$. Fuzzy derivatives of both languages and restricted regular
expressions arise
(Definitions~\ref{def:fuzzy-deriv-lang} and~\ref{def:fuzzy-deriv-expr}). The idea
that makes the layer work is simple. Under the minimum
T-norm, cut-similarity is an equivalence relation
(Proposition~\ref{prop:cut-equivalence}). Once that is in hand, a fuzzy derivative
over $\Sigma$ is just an ordinary derivative over the quotient alphabet
$\Sigma^{\mu}$ obtained by collapsing $\mu$-similar symbols into a single class
(Theorem~\ref{the:quotient-fuzzy-derivative}). This quotient picture lets
the crisp theory carry over, so regularity
(Theorem~\ref{the:fuzzy-regular}), the correspondence between syntax and semantics
(Theorem~\ref{the:fuzzy-correspondence}), finiteness
(Theorem~\ref{the:fuzzy-finite}) and fuzzy DFA correctness
(Theorem~\ref{the:fuzzy-dfa}) all follow. The fuzzy automaton
accepts the words that come within threshold $\mu$ of some word in the
language.

The crisp theory is formalised in Rocq. It covers the
syntax and semantics, nullability, canonicalisation and its correctness, the
derivative correspondence and DFA correctness, and it turns these claims into
statements a proof assistant has checked. The similarity infrastructure that the
fuzzy results build on is formalised there as well. The next step is to settle the
Kuratowski-finiteness result for canonical derivatives. After that the fuzzy layer
can be implemented, including the construction of a fuzzy derivative automaton.
